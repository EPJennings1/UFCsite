    History

Origins (1991–1993)

The UFC began as an idea developed by Art Davie, a businessman interested in staging a real contest between different martial arts disciplines. He partnered with Rorion Gracie, a co-founder of Gracie Jiu-Jitsu, and Robert Meyrowitz, head of Semaphore Entertainment Group (SEG). Their goal was to create a tournament that would showcase the most effective martial art in real combat.The result was UFC 1, held on November 12, 1993, in Denver, Colorado. The event featured minimal rules, no weight classes, and fighters from various backgrounds. Royce Gracie’s early dominance introduced Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu to a global audience and shaped the foundations of MMA.



Patrick Smith and Ken Shamrock, UFC 1

Early Turbulence and Regulation (1994–2000)

Following its debut, the UFC quickly grew in popularity but also attracted criticism due to its violent presentation. Politicians such as Senator John McCain openly opposed the events, calling them “human cockfighting.” Under increasing pressure, many states banned the UFC, and cable companies dropped pay-per-view support. During this period, SEG attempted to rebrand the sport by adding rules, weight classes, and rounds. Despite these efforts, financial instability grew, putting the future of the organization at risk.



Pedro Rizzo and Tank Abbott, UFC Ultimate

The Zuffa Era Begins (2001–2016)

In 2001, Lorenzo and Frank Fertitta acquired the struggling UFC through their company Zuffa LLC andappointed Dana White as president. This executive team modernized the organization through:

  • Adoption of the Unified Rules of Mixed Martial Arts
  • Expansion of weight classes
  • Stronger athletic commission oversight
  • Improved production, promotion, and fighter marketing
A pivotal moment came in 2005 with the debut of The Ultimate Fighter on Spike TV. The season’s finale, featuring Forrest Griffin vs. Stephan Bonnar, is widely credited with catapulting the UFC into mainstream popularity.Throughout this period, major stars such as Chuck Liddell, Randy Couture, Tito Ortiz, Matt Hughes, Georges St-Pierre, Anderson Silva, Brock Lesnar, and Jon Jones helped fuel rapid growth. The UFC expanded globally, adding new divisions and hosting events across Europe, Australia, Brazil, Canada, and Asia.



UFC President, Dana White

Expansion, New Stars, and the Modern Sporting Era (2010–2016)

The UFC continued to grow into a polished global sports brand. New champions such as José Aldo, Cain Velasquez, Ronda Rousey, and Demetrious Johnson brought visibility to lighter weight classes and women’s MMA. The emergence of Conor McGregor and Khabib Nurmagomedov produced some of the biggest pay-per-view events in history. During this time, the UFC also signed broadcast deals with Fox Sports and expanded its digital footprint through UFC Fight Pass.



Conor McGregor and Khabib Nurmagomedov, UFC 229

Acquisition by Endeavor (2016–Present)

In 2016, Zuffa sold the UFC to WME-IMG (now Endeavor) in a landmark multi-billion-dollar deal. Executives Ari Emanuel and Patrick Whitesell took significant leadership roles while Dana White remained as president. Under Endeavor, the UFC:

  • Signed a major broadcast partnership with ESPN beginning in 2019
  • Opened UFC Performance Institutes in Las Vegas, Shanghai, and Mexico City
  • Expanded its global roster and strengthened development programs
  • Continued to produce record-setting events with stars such as Amanda Nunes, Israel Adesanya, Kamaru Usman, Alexander Volkanovski, Sean O’Malley, and others
The organization has grown into the premier MMA promotion worldwide, becoming a centerpiece of global combat sports and a dominant player in pay-per-view entertainment.



Ilia Topuria and Charles Oliveira, UFC 317

Legacy and Cultural Impact

The UFC transformed mixed martial arts from a fringe spectacle into a regulated, internationally respected sport. Through pioneering executives, rule changes, and the rise of elite fighters across multiple eras, the UFC helped shape MMA into a mainstream global industry. Today, it remains the standard-bearer for professional MMA competition and continues to influence martial arts culture, training, and media worldwide.
